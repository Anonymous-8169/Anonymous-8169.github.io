Abstract

Robotic manipulation in cluttered, human-centered spaces requires more than fingertip sensing: robots must also detect and regulate contact along the arm. We present two complementary whole-arm tactile skin architectures that are low-cost, practical to fabricate, and compatible with mobile manipulators. The textile sensor is optimized for low-force sensitivity and contact avoidance, while the additive-manufacturing (AM) sensor is optimized for higher-force, durable contact embracing. Both are based on resistive taxel matrices and provide analog force measurements. We contribute fabrication guidelines, tunable design parameters, and quantitative characterization of operating force range, repeatability, and hysteresis. Real-world experiments on Spot show that tactile feedback enables deliberate and safe whole-body interaction beyond simple collision avoidance.

Core Contributions

  • Two complementary whole-arm force-sensing skin architectures: a textile design and an AM conformal-shell design.
  • Low-cost, high-coverage fabrication workflows with practical deployment on commercial robot links.
  • Quantitative characterization across operating force range, repeatability, and hysteresis.
  • Real-world task validation for both contact avoidance and contact embracing behaviors.

Sensor Architectures

Textile Sensor

  • Flexible sleeve with conductive fabric rows and columns separated by Velostat.
  • Near-zero detection threshold in flat configuration and strong low-force sensitivity.
  • Best suited for contact avoidance and light-touch interaction.
  • Fabrication requires only simple textile tooling (for example, a fabric iron).

AM Sensor

  • 3D-printed conformal inner and outer TPU shells with ridge-defined taxels and Velostat.
  • Normally open structure reduces cross-talk and improves localization robustness.
  • Best suited for contact embracing and higher-force, durable interaction.
  • Generated from link geometry for direct deployment on complex robot surfaces.

Modeling and Characterization

Each taxel is measured through a voltage-divider circuit and mapped to force using a calibrated power-law resistance model with base resistance. We evaluate: (1) operating force range from detection threshold to saturation, (2) multi-force repeatability using coefficient of variation across cycles, and (3) hysteresis via loading-unloading discrepancy.

Real-World Use Cases

Contact Avoidance (Textile)

The textile skin detects light, unexpected contact and drives contact-aware whole-body inverse kinematics to move the contacted link away while preserving the primary end-effector goal.

Contact Embracing (AM)

The AM skin supports controlled, higher-force interaction such as bracing on a door while balancing a tool, enabling stable force regulation during task execution.

Limitations and Future Work

  • Wiring complexity scales with taxel count and is challenging around rotary axes.
  • Improved compensation is needed for hysteresis and temperature-induced drift.
  • Future versions will explore embedded processing, simplified interconnects, and expanded material tuning.
